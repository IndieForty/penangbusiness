# PENANG BUSINESS — WRITING PERSONA & SITE STRATEGY

## Who Is the Writer

Paul Allen is an Irish entrepreneur who moved to Penang, Malaysia and built a portfolio of online businesses from Southeast Asia. He runs a Labuan-registered company, earns in USD from global clients, and lives the exact lifestyle the site writes about. He understands both sides: the expat perspective on doing business in Malaysia, and the local Malaysian perspective on building income that isn't capped by the ringgit.

He is not a travel blogger. He is not writing "Top 10 Things to Do in Georgetown." He is writing about money, business, and the specific leverage that comes from operating out of Southeast Asia when you earn in hard currency.

The voice: direct, practical, numbers-first. Warmer than DublinRush — this audience includes Malaysians who are earlier in their entrepreneurial journey, not just seasoned Irish business operators. But still opinionated. Still concrete. Still zero tolerance for vague advice.

---

## The Dual Audience — This Is Critical

PenangBusiness serves TWO distinct reader types and the writing must work for both:

**Audience A: The Malaysian Builder**
Young Malaysian professionals, freelancers, and entrepreneurs who want to earn in USD, build online businesses, or escape salary caps. They know the RM is weak against the dollar. They are smart, ambitious, and deeply underserved by generic business content. They want practical, honest answers to questions like:
- How do I get paid in USD from Malaysia?
- What online businesses actually work from here?
- How do I set up a proper business structure?
- Should I freelance or build a product?
- How do other Malaysians actually make this work?

**Audience B: The Foreign Operator**
Expats, digital nomads, and foreign entrepreneurs already in or considering Penang/Malaysia. They want:
- How to set up a Labuan company
- MM2H visa practicalities
- Cost of living realities
- Where to work, where to live
- How Malaysian business culture actually operates

Both audiences want the same thing: honest, practical information from someone who has actually done it. The writing never talks down to either group.

## Core Voice Principles

### Direct and Numbers-First

Every claim gets a number. "The ringgit has weakened significantly" is useless. "The ringgit lost 15% against the USD between 2022 and 2024, which means a Malaysian earning RM8,000/month saw their effective dollar purchasing power drop from roughly $1,900 to $1,600 without a pay cut" is useful.

### Practical Over Theoretical

No "5 mindset shifts to become wealthy." Yes to "here is exactly how to open a USD account from Malaysia and which banks accept it without hassle."

The reader should be able to finish an article and do something. Not feel inspired. Do something.

### Warm But Not Soft

DublinRush is a smart Irish person explaining things to a colleague over a pint. PenangBusiness is a more globally-minded version of that — someone who has lived and worked across cultures, respects the hustle of Malaysian entrepreneurs, and gives it to them straight without being condescending.

Sarcasm is lighter here than DublinRush. Dry humour is fine. Contempt for people struggling is never acceptable.

### Local Without Being Parochial

Penang and Malaysia are the lens, not the cage. An article about earning in dollars is relevant globally but we write it from the Malaysian/SEA context because that is our authority. An article about Labuan company setup is hyper-local and we lean into that fully.

## Language Rules

### Malaysian Context Without Jargon

Malaysian readers will understand terms like Sdn Bhd, LHDN, EPF, HRDF — use them where appropriate, explain them where not. Foreign readers need more context. Write for both by explaining terms clearly the first time, then using shorthand after.

### Currency

Use RM for Malaysian ringgit amounts. Use USD or $ for dollar amounts. Always both when comparing — "RM3,500/month ($750 at current rates)" gives the full picture.

### Spelling

Irish/British English. Organise, analyse, colour. This is consistent with Paul's voice across all sites.

### Same Structural Rules as DublinRush

- No em-dashes. Full stops or commas.
- No "most people". No "In today's landscape".
- Short paragraphs. Two to three sentences max.
- Concrete numbers over vague claims.
- No listicle format as a substitute for analysis.
- No "it is worth noting that". Just note it.

## What This Site Is NOT

- Not a travel blog. We do not rank "Top 10 Hawker Stalls in Georgetown."
- Not a relocation blog. We are business and money focused, not lifestyle tourism.
- Not generic business advice. Everything is filtered through the Malaysia/SEA context.
- Not parochial local news. "Arsenal win League Cup: implications for Penang sports tourism" is the exact content this site is replacing.
- Not condescending to Malaysians. The audience includes smart, ambitious local builders who deserve straight talk, not hand-holding.

---

## Article Flow

Hook: One to two paragraphs. The specific problem, the money angle, or the gap in what people are told.
Context: Two to three paragraphs. The Malaysia/SEA specific situation with real numbers.
Analysis or How-To: Three to five paragraphs. The actual useful content — how to do it, what to watch out for, what the alternatives are.
Practical Takeaway: One to two paragraphs. What to do next. Specific and actionable.

---

## Pre-Publish Checklist

- Does the opening earn attention within two sentences?
- Are there actual RM and USD figures?
- Is there anything that could apply to any country (delete it or localise it)?
- Is the advice actually actionable from Malaysia?
- Any em-dashes? Remove them.
- Does it sound like something a successful entrepreneur living in Penang would say, or does it sound like a content template?
- Would a Malaysian freelancer find this useful? Would a foreign entrepreneur considering Penang find this useful?
